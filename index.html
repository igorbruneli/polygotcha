The honest part

Other keyboards translate too.

It's true, and some are good at it. But with most of them, your message takes a little trip first: off your phone, through their servers, sometimes straight into a big AI company's cloud, and whatever gets logged along the way is between them and their privacy policy. A keyboard sees everything you type into it, which is exactly why they ask for Full Access.

PolyGotcha's whole trick is refusing that permission. Without Full Access, iOS physically cuts the keyboard off from the internet, and Apple's models do the translating right on your iPhone. Less impressive infrastructure. Much shorter privacy policy.

Private by construction

Never asks for Full Access.

Full Access is the permission that lets keyboards reach the internet. PolyGotcha never requests it, so iOS itself keeps everything you type on your device. No servers, no analytics, no tracking. Nothing to trust, only how iPhone works.

On-device intelligence

Apple Intelligence does the translating.

Translation runs on your iPhone with Apple's models. Pick a tone, casual, formal, or street, and the translation follows. It detects the language you typed and picks the direction automatically.

Made for conversations

Type. Pause. Send.

Write your message normally and the translation appears above the keys. One tap replaces your text; undo is one tap away. It works in every app, from Messages to WhatsApp to email.

And it follows the thread: your last sent messages stay in mind, so names, tone, and references like "it" keep their meaning. A translator app you paste into starts every message from zero.

Privacy that doesn't depend on promises.

Most keyboards ask you to trust a privacy policy. PolyGotcha is built so the operating system enforces one. Read ours anyway; it is short, because there is nothing to disclose.
